TEACHING

Teaching Philosophy

Maria Montessori reminds us that “it is not enough for the teacher to love the child. She must first love and understand the universe.” I am infinitely curious about the way cultures, languages, history, and society shape the educational enterprise, and make it a point to explore issues in education and the world around us with my students. For example, in courses preparing students to be social studies educators, we view films such as Precious Knowledge, Older Than America, and War Don Don in order to explore historical and contemporary issues surrounding social studies education. We also visit community sites of learning, attending PTA meetings, afterschool programs, and extracurricular activities salient to student civic learning. To help students have an embodied experience of learning in an unknown language, a colleague and I developed a set of instructional videos in Twi, a language unknown to most students, to demonstrate the importance of sheltered or bilingual instruction.
